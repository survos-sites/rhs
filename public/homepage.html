A New Chapter

This Organization Almost Disappeared

After the passing of John and Judy Tole—the beloved leaders who guided this society for decades—within four months of each other, the Rappahannock Historical Society faced an uncertain future.

The last surviving board member, 86-year-old Lilian Aylor, was sought out at her home in Winchester to sign the resolution that would allow the nonprofit to reorganize. She passed away just two weeks later.

Thanks to a group of dedicated locals who recognized what was at stake, the Society has been reborn. With Michael Dennis as interim board president and Eva Grimsley—whose family has been in this county for generations—as executive director, we look forward to our next, vibrant phase.

Stories to Tell

Family Files

Over a thousand Rappahannock County families documented—from a few pages to extensive multi-file collections spanning generations.

Cemetery Project

Every graveyard in the county documented. Volunteers gathered names from tiny family plots hidden among trees and behind crumbling stone walls.

Oral Histories

The memories of lifelong county residents—transcribed interviews capturing the lived experience of Rappahannock through the decades.

Property Deeds

Trace property ownership back through Rappahannock, Culpeper, and Orange counties—centuries of land records at your fingertips.

Museum Artifacts

Items spanning from prehistoric times to the 20th century, including Civil War relics and period clothing from local stores.

Digital Archives

We're digitizing our collections for broader access. Explore photographs, documents, and records through our online archive system.

Building Bridges

Telling the Complete History of Rappahannock

The more we learn about one another, the stronger we become as a community. Under new leadership, the Society is forging partnerships to ensure that all of Rappahannock's history—the good and the difficult—is preserved and shared.

"Those stone walls running through fields were built by my ancestors. I want to know more about those people. We can all learn from their experience here."
— Lois Goodall, African American Association for Rappahannock County

We've maintained records of enslaved people named in wills and sales documents. Now we're partnering with AAARC to bring forth true history that has been overlooked and ignored—because everyone's story deserves to be told.
